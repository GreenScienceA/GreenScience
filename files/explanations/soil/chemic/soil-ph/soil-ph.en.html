‚öóÔ∏è Sources of Acidity and Alkalinity in Soils

Why Do Soils Become Acid?

Soil acidification is a natural and ongoing process driven by multiple mechanisms. Understanding these sources is essential for management:

1. Carbonic acid from respiration. Roots and microorganisms produce CO‚ÇÇ through respiration. CO‚ÇÇ dissolves in soil water to form carbonic acid (H‚ÇÇCO‚ÇÉ), which dissociates to release H‚Å∫. This is the primary natural acidification pathway in most soils (Sposito, 2008).

2. Organic acid production. Decomposition of organic matter generates organic acids (fulvic, humic, oxalic, citric) that donate H‚Å∫ to the soil solution. Forest litter is particularly acidifying.

3. Nitrification of ammonium fertilizers. When NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ is oxidized to NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª by soil bacteria (Nitrosomonas and Nitrobacter), two H‚Å∫ ions are released per molecule of NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ oxidized. This is the dominant cause of anthropogenic soil acidification worldwide: NH‚ÇÑ‚Å∫ + 2O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí NO‚ÇÉ‚Åª + H‚ÇÇO + 2H‚Å∫ (Bolan et al., 2003).

4. Leaching of base cations. Rainfall (naturally slightly acidic at pH ‚âà 5.6 due to dissolved CO‚ÇÇ) percolating through the soil carries away Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Mg¬≤‚Å∫, K‚Å∫, and Na‚Å∫ ‚Äî the base cations that buffer acidity. Over centuries, this depletes the soil's buffering capacity and drives pH downward. This process is accelerated in high-rainfall humid regions (Sparks, 2003).

5. Acid rain. Industrial emissions of SO‚ÇÇ and NO‚Çì form sulfuric and nitric acids in the atmosphere, which can lower precipitation pH to 4.0‚Äì4.5. While regulations have reduced acid rain in many regions, its cumulative legacy persists in forest soils (Ulrich et al., 1980).

6. Oxidation of sulfide minerals. When soils containing pyrite (FeS‚ÇÇ) are drained or exposed to air ‚Äî as in acid sulfate soils or mine tailings ‚Äî the sulfide oxidizes to produce sulfuric acid, driving pH as low as 2.0‚Äì3.0. These are among the most acidic soils on Earth.

Why Do Soils Become Alkaline?

1. Carbonate minerals. Soils formed from limestone or chalk parent materials contain CaCO‚ÇÉ that dissolves to release OH‚Åª equivalents, buffering pH between 7.5 and 8.5. Common in semi-arid and arid regions including the S√©tif highlands.

2. Low rainfall / high evaporation. In arid climates, insufficient rainfall means base cations are not leached and salts accumulate at or near the surface, maintaining high pH.

3. Sodium accumulation (sodicity). When Na‚Å∫ dominates the exchange complex (ESP > 15%), soil pH can rise above 8.5, dispersing clay aggregates and creating impermeable surface crusts. Sodic soils are among the most degraded on Earth.

üõ°Ô∏è Buffering Capacity: Why Soil Resists pH Change

Soil buffering capacity is the ability of a soil to resist changes in pH when acid or base is added. It is one of the most practically important soil properties because it determines how much lime is needed to correct acidity and how rapidly fertilizers will re-acidify the soil.

The main buffering mechanisms are:

1. Cation exchange. Clay minerals and organic matter carry negative charges that hold exchangeable base cations (Ca¬≤‚Å∫, Mg¬≤‚Å∫, K‚Å∫). When H‚Å∫ is added, it displaces these cations ‚Äî absorbing the acid without changing pH until the exchange sites are saturated.

2. Dissolution/precipitation of carbonates. In calcareous soils, CaCO‚ÇÉ dissolves to neutralize acid: CaCO‚ÇÉ + 2H‚Å∫ ‚Üí Ca¬≤‚Å∫ + H‚ÇÇO + CO‚ÇÇ. This keeps pH locked near 7.5‚Äì8.5 as long as free CaCO‚ÇÉ remains.

3. Aluminum hydrolysis. Below pH 5.5, aluminum dissolves from clay minerals and undergoes a series of hydrolysis reactions that consume H‚Å∫, buffering pH near 4.0‚Äì5.0: Al¬≥‚Å∫ + H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí Al(OH)¬≤‚Å∫ + H‚Å∫. In most mineral soils, sufficient Al is present to buffer pH around 4.0 (Sposito, 2008).

4. Organic matter functional groups. Carboxyl (‚àíCOOH) and phenolic (‚àíOH) groups on humus act as weak acids, accepting or donating protons and buffering across a wide pH range. Soils high in organic matter have outstanding buffering capacity.

As a general rule: high-CEC soils (clays, organic soils) have strong buffering capacity and require more lime to change pH, while low-CEC soils (sands) have weak buffering and respond quickly to both acidification and liming (Brady & Weil, 2017).

üî¨ Measuring Soil pH

pH in Water (pHH‚ÇÇO)

The standard method: mix soil with distilled water at a 1:2.5 (soil:water) ratio, stir, let settle, and measure with a glass electrode pH meter. This gives the active acidity ‚Äî the H‚Å∫ actually in solution (Thomas, 1996).

The relationship: pHKCl < pHH‚ÇÇO (always). The difference (ŒîpH = pHH‚ÇÇO ‚àí pHKCl) indicates the reserve acidity ‚Äî a larger gap means more exchangeable Al¬≥‚Å∫ and H‚Å∫ on clay surfaces.

Raising pH: Liming Acid Soils

Liming is the most common soil amendment practice on Earth. The reaction is elegantly simple:

CaCO‚ÇÉ + 2H‚Å∫ ‚Üí Ca¬≤‚Å∫ + H‚ÇÇO + CO‚ÇÇ‚Üë

Calcium carbonate neutralizes soil acidity, raises pH, eliminates Al¬≥‚Å∫ toxicity, and supplies Ca¬≤‚Å∫ to the exchange complex. A recent meta-analysis by Enesi et al. (2023) covering 261 field studies confirmed that liming consistently increases crop yields on acid soils, with an average yield increase of 33% ‚Äî making it one of the most cost-effective soil management practices available.

Common liming materials include calcitic limestone (CaCO‚ÇÉ), dolomitic limestone (CaMg(CO‚ÇÉ)‚ÇÇ ‚Äî also supplies Mg), burnt lime (CaO ‚Äî fast-acting but caustic), and agricultural waste materials such as wood ash.

Lowering pH: Acidifying Alkaline Soils

Acidification is much harder and more expensive than liming because the CaCO‚ÇÉ buffer in calcareous soils is massive. Elemental sulfur (S‚Å∞) is the most common acidifying agent:

2S‚Å∞ + 3O‚ÇÇ + 2H‚ÇÇO ‚Üí 2H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ ‚Üí 4H‚Å∫ + 2SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª

For calcareous soils, agronomic strategies ‚Äî chelated micronutrient applications, organic matter, localized P banding, acidifying fertilizers ‚Äî are usually more practical than trying to lower bulk soil pH (Brady & Weil, 2017).

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Misconceptions

1. "pH 7 is always ideal." Not for all crops. Blueberries thrive at pH 4.5‚Äì5.5. Potatoes grow best at pH 5.0‚Äì5.5 (and develop scab disease at higher pH). Azaleas, rhododendrons, and tea require acid soils. The "ideal" pH depends entirely on the crop (Brady & Weil, 2017).

2. "A small pH change doesn't matter." Because pH is logarithmic, a shift from pH 6 to pH 5 means a tenfold increase in H‚Å∫ concentration. A shift from 6 to 4 is a hundredfold increase. Small numbers, massive chemical consequences.

3. "Adding fertilizer can fix pH problems." Fertilizer provides nutrients but does not change pH ‚Äî in fact, ammonium-based fertilizers (urea, ammonium sulfate) accelerate acidification. Only liming materials (CaCO‚ÇÉ, dolomite) can raise acid soil pH sustainably.

4. "Calcareous soils just need more fertilizer." Adding more P fertilizer to a soil at pH 8 is wasteful ‚Äî the P will be immediately precipitated as insoluble Ca-phosphates. The solution is not more fertilizer but smarter placement (banding), organic amendments, or chelated forms that resist fixation.

5. "The Truog diagram is precise and universal." Truog himself emphasized it was conceptual and tentative. Real nutrient availability depends on many factors beyond pH: organic matter, redox status, microbial activity, root exudates, and soil mineralogy. The diagram shows trends, not absolute predictions (Hartemink & Barrow, 2023).

üß© Summary: pH as the Master Key to Soil Understanding

From S√∏rensen's beer laboratory in 1909 to the satellite-mapped pH surveys of the 21st century, soil pH has proven to be the most powerful single predictor of soil chemical behavior. It governs nutrient availability through the classic Truog relationships, controls aluminum toxicity that threatens crops on 30% of the world's arable land, shapes microbial communities that drive decomposition and nutrient cycling, and determines whether amendments like lime or sulfur are needed.

For the agronomist, pH is the first diagnostic ‚Äî the master key that unlocks the interpretation of all other soil test results. For the ecologist, it is the environmental filter that structures microbial and plant communities across biomes. For the environmental scientist, it controls the mobility of heavy metals and the fate of pollutants. Learning to read soil pH ‚Äî and understanding the buffering mechanisms, the logarithmic scale, the three pools of acidity, and the nutrient availability patterns it controls ‚Äî is one of the most valuable skills in all of soil science.

As Brady and Weil wrote in what is perhaps the definitive textbook of modern soil science: soil pH is not just a measurement ‚Äî it is a window into the soul of the soil.
